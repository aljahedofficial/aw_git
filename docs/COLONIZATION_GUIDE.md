## What NOT to Change

### L2-Authentic Grammatical Structures

These are VALUABLE and shouldn't be erased:

- **Topic-prominent sentences**: "This writing assignment, I found very difficult"
- **Serial verb constructions**: "I study hard make good grades"
- **Cultural metaphors**: References to your home country, family values, traditions
- **L1 patterns**: These show your authentic background, not errors
- **Personal anecdotes**: Stories from your own experience

**The tool highlights these!** When it shows "Voice Loss Alerts," it's flagging something you should probably keep.

### What CAN Be Changed

Safe edits that won't colonize your voice:

- Grammar corrections (subject-verb agreement, tense consistency)
- Spelling fixes
- Clarity improvements (e.g., "The book was complex and had many ideas" â†’ "The book explored complex themes")
- Adding transitions between paragraphs you already wrote
- Breaking long sentences into shorter ones (using your own words)

## L2-Specific Features

### L2 Voice Preservation Engine

This tool specifically recognizes that L2 writers have legitimate, valuable linguistic patterns that are NOT errors:

#### What It Protects:

1. **Grammatical Structures**
   - Object-Verb word order (common in Asian languages)
   - Aspect marking (how you express time/action type)
   - Topic-prominent sentences

2. **Cultural Language**
   - Family-based expressions
   - Nature metaphors from your region
   - Proverbs and cultural wisdom
   - Historical references meaningful to you

3. **L1 Transfer Patterns**
   - Emphasis patterns ("very very important")
   - Formality shifts (mixing formal and casual)
   - Metaphors influenced by your L1

### Why This Matters

Generic American academic English is NOT the only valid way to write. Your unique linguistic background is a STRENGTH, not a weakness. The analyzer helps you preserve:

- **Your voice**: What makes your writing distinctly yours
- **Your identity**: How you as an L2 writer uniquely see the world
- **Your learning**: Your essay shows your actual language development
- **Your agency**: You decide how to balance AI help with authentic expression

## Tips for Ethical AI Use in Writing

### âœ“ DO Use AI For:
- Grammar checking (if you review each suggestion)
- Vocabulary alternatives (pick ones that fit your voice)
- Structure suggestions (use if they improve clarity)
- Citation formatting
- Brainstorming ideas

### âœ— DON'T Use AI For:
- Wholesale rewriting
- Replacing your voice with generic language
- Writing sections you don't understand
- Avoiding expressing your own thoughts
- Hiding how you actually think/write

### Better Practice:
1. **Write your draft first** in your authentic voice
2. **Use AI for targeted help** on specific words/sentences
3. **Review every AI suggestion** and ask: "Is this still me?"
4. **Keep your original phrasing** when it's clear and correct
5. **Use this tool to check** that you're still the author of your essay

## FAQ

### Q: Is my score good?
**A:** It depends on your intention. If you wanted AI just for polishing, 80+ is great. If your instructor asked you to write in your authentic voice, anything under 70 might be a concern.

### Q: What if I got a low score?
**A:** You have options:
- Go back to your original draft
- Make smaller edits yourself
- Restore key phrases from your draft to the AI version
- Ask: "Do I want this essay to sound like me?"

### Q: Can I improve my score?
**A:** Yes! You can manually:
- Replace generic phrases with your original wording
- Restore cultural references and metaphors
- Add back L2-authentic structures the AI removed
- Insert more first-person voice (pronouns, personal examples)

### Q: What if the score says my voice was colonized?
**A:** This means the AI heavily rewrote your essay. Consider:
- **Was this necessary?** Did the edits actually improve clarity, or just "sound fancier"?
- **Is this ethical?** Submitting work that doesn't sound like you might violate academic integrity
- **What can you restore?** Can you keep the improvements while re-adding your voice?

### Q: Can I use this tool to understand my own voice better?
**A:** Absolutely! Many students use it to discover:
- What patterns they use frequently
- How their L1 influences their English
- What makes their writing distinctive
- How much is "their voice" vs. learned academic conventions

### Q: Is there a minimum text length?
**A:** The tool works best with 150+ words. Shorter pieces may not show clear patterns.
